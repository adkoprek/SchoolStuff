# Pages 3–25

## Recapitulation

Winston is the protagonist, and the story is told from his point of view.

The story takes place in a dystopian world.

Big Brother is watching you through telescreens.

There are four ministries that rule as a dictatorship, named with euphemisms:
Ministry of Love → maintains law and order (and is terrifying).

The party slogan is not questioned by the protagonist.

They accept war and peace as synonyms.

He writes a diary for future generations.

They cannot tell the date with more precision than two years.

The society is amused by brutal war violence.

Two Minutes of Hate, which are part of the brainwashing by the ruling party, are supposed to act as propaganda against the enemies of the system.

Goldstein is the most hated one — an advocate for human rights who gained Big Brother’s trust and then betrayed him.

Big Brother, portrayed as the savior on the screen, is worshiped through songs and chanting.

O’Brien and Winston looked at each other, and Winston knew from his expression that O’Brien was also against the propaganda of the Two Minutes of Hate.

Just the written words against the system made him fear for his life — until someone knocked on the door.

At the door was the neighbor, who asked for his help to repair the sink.

The children of the neighbor played military games and pretended that Winston was a spy, acting as if they were killing him.

The children really wanted to see the hanging of a person, as if it were an attraction.

## Our Opinion

It seems interesting.

It seems absurd, even for a dystopian novel.

Very early in the story, the protagonist is surprisingly worried about his life.

We think that Winston just has bursts of bravery.

We think he is bad with people (like everyone else).

Winston is especially bad with women — that’s why he dislikes them.
