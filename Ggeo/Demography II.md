## Social Security in Switzerland

### You can explain the general structure of the Swiss retirement system

The Swiss retirement system is based on three pillars, which combined are supposed to give a citizen a good retirement fund for the rest of their life.

- The `AHV` or the old age and survivors insurance, also called state pension,  is the first pillar of the retirement system is received by all retired residents over the age of 65. All citizens have to pay into the AHV and then receive money from this fund in their retirement to secure their survival. The money that a citizen gets from the AHV has a minimum and maximum cap, but the payments are always proportional to the income and are not capped. There exists a point where an increase in contributions does not increase the AHV.

- The second pillar is the occupational pension and is there to maintain one's current standard of living and is paid by the employee and the employer into a personal fund that can be accessed at retirement.

- The third pillar is a private pension that is for individual supplements is personal funding and is not mandatory.

### You can explain and apply the basic principles of solidarity underlying the first pillar (AHV) and its funding mechanisms

Retired people rely on the working citizens to provide them with money to life. The working citizens are only willing to give up this piece of their income because thy all believe that the future generations are going to do the same for them. This mechanism is called the solidarity principle.

### You can identify and explain the factors that influence AHV funding and describe how the system operates

The AHV is financed through VAT (Value Added Tax), Tobacco, Alcohol & Casino Taxes. But mainly it is funded using the salary contributions where every employee pays 4.35% of his own salary for the AHV (5.3% for all social insurances) and the employee pays the same amount as well. This leads that effectively per employee, the AHV receives 8.7% (10.6% for all insurances) of their pay check. This money is then paid into a pot and collected and then distributes to the retired people proportionally to how much they paid in. The AHV on the other hand is bounded between 1260 CHF and 2520 CHF, which leads to the fact that rich people contribute more than they get from the AHV.

## One Child Policy

### You can describe how China’s One-Child Policy was designed and implemented up until its abolition in 2015

As the Chinese population was moving from the second to the third stage of the demographic transition model (DTM) it experienced a gigantic population gain in the last stage because death rates have fallen drastically but people, still not used to the new standard of living, produced the same amount of babies. This rate of births started to decline between the 60s and 70s.

As the Chinese government was afraid of overpopulation, it wanted to artificially change the birth rates by enforcing that a family in China was only allowed to have one child.

### You can explain the current and future impacts of the One-Child Policy on China’s society, economy, and demographics

This drastic and unnatural change in the behavior of humans had drastic impacts on the age structure of the Chinese population, which are now apparent. 

In the time of the implementation, the policy has produced a temporary relief on the economy and society of the Chinese population by lowering by how much the population grows. As the birth rates of China dropped below the replacement level it is now apparent, that China is not able to support the older population which now becomes retired and outweighs the younger population drastically. Therefore, creating a lot of social friction by creating labor shortages and slow economic growth.

## Thomas Malthus

### You can explain the basic idea presented by Thomas Malthus roughly 200 years ago, using appropriate terminology

Thomas noted correctly already 200 years ago that population growth could be approximated exponentially. He also assumed that food production would continue linearly. As he was afraid of overpopulation leading to famines, he drew the conclusion that population growth had to be regulated, otherwise the human population would pass the point that could be supported by the slowly growing agriculture production. This point is called the Malthusian trap.

In his time he claimed that the irresponsibility of the lower class which produced too much offspring which eventually caused lower standards of living. Therefore, he suggested that the family size should be regulated by late marriage, chastity and moral restraint.

### You are able to explain how his ideas relate to China’s One-Child Policy.

As China went through the great leap forward where it was industrialized which lead that most population was forced to move from the agriculture to the industrial sector. This led to not enough food being produced. This then led to a gigantic famine, which caused a big spike in child mortality.

So the Chinese population was well afraid of overpopulation, which led them to implement a policy, similar to Malthus idea. They therefore implemented the one child policy to combat the gigantic increase in population artificially. The key difference to Malthus idea is that they forced this decision onto all citizens.

### You can evaluate Malthus’s ideas from a historical and contemporary perspective

From today's standpoint, we know that Malthus predictions were inaccurate. Malthus did not consider that innovative technology through industrialization would allow the food production to grow as well exponentially and to support the population.

As we have seen in China, this idea does not prevent famines but creates not enough new population, which causes that there more old people who can not be supported by the much smaller younger population. Whereas in some countries we see even underpopulation.

## Earthquakes

### You know about the events and impacts of the Great Hanshin Earthquake in Kobe (1995), including its causes and consequences

The earthquake occurred at 5:45 am on the 17th of January in 1995 in Kobe in Japan and had a magnitude of 7.3 on the Richter scale and lasted only 20 sec. The short duration caused that the energy was deposited in a very short time because of this the earthquake felt very intense.

The damage included electricity and water cut out, burning gas pipes, collapsed buildings, cracked and twisted roads paired with overwhelmed and not properly trained officials.

It was caused by 3 plates that came together in one point. Which was amplified by the narrow landscape of Japan where cities are fit between mountains and oceans and can only be expanded by building more reclaimed land artificially which collapses easily during earthquakes.

### You can explain important key terms

- The `offset` is the distance between the old and new position of the Earth's surface after an Earthquake.

- The `focus` is the location beneath the earth surface where rock breaks and triggers seismic waves.

- The `epicenter` is the projection of the focus onto the earths surface.

- The `liquefaction` is the process when, in earthquakes, loosely packed sediments lose friction and the ground starts to behave like a liquid. The shaking causes that small gaps between the small rocks in the ground are created and water can enter these gaps which leads that the friction between those rocks is weekend.

- `Seismic waves` are waves that travel through the earths surface from the focus and carry some of the energy released in an earthquake.

- The `magnitude` is the measure of the energy released during an earthquake.

- The `resonance` is a process when waves have the same frequency as the natural frequency of an object and hit it, causing it to swing.

- The theory of the `elastic rebound` is that tectonic plates which have fractured at one point spring back to their original shape, which releases a lot of energy quickly and therefore causes an earthquake.

### You can explain and assess the role of site effects on the impact of an earthquake

Site effects describe the geological formation at a specific place, which causes that places with the same distance from the epicenter experience different impacts of the earthquake.

This happens because seismic waves travel faster through hard rock and slower through soft soils (factor of nearly 10). This creates harder shaking in soft soils, which causes more damage.

### You can explain some approaches used in earthquake engineering to minimize casualties on buildings

- A `tuned mass damper` is tuned to the same natural frequency as a building and is freely suspended. Should the building be exited by seismic waves at the resonance frequency of the building, it starts oscillating. Due to the fact that the pendulum is suspended freely, it moves in the opposite direction of the building, therefore stabilizing it and absorbing some energy.

- A `foundation` to prevent liquefaction includes a fine and deep mesh of pillars going deep into the ground and prevent the earth to move around during an earthquake, which prevents gaps in the soil from opening up and allowing for liquefaction.
